# Galley Brand System — V1

## 1. Brand idea

**Galley** is the supervised content-operations system for agencies and DTC teams.

It turns client playbooks into proof-ready content, checks every draft, routes work through a human approval gate, schedules approved deliverables, and preserves a record of every decision.

**Tagline:**  
Proof before press.

**Core promise:**  
Content operations without the chaos.

**Expanded promise:**  
Galley gives agencies a governed production line for client content: produce, verify, proof, schedule, and report — with every draft, edit, approval, and publish recorded.

## 2. Brand posture

Galley should feel like an editorial command desk, not an AI toy.

The brand is:

* precise
* calm
* operational
* premium
* editorial
* trustworthy
* modern
* restrained
* quietly technical

The brand is not:

* magical
* gimmicky
* neon
* cartoonish
* over-animated
* “10x bro SaaS”
* generic AI gradient
* fake dashboard theatre
* prompt-box-first

## 3. Positioning

### One-line positioning

Galley is a supervised content-operations agent for agencies and DTC teams.

### Homepage positioning

Proof-ready content operations for agencies.

Galley produces, verifies, proofs, schedules, and reports client content — with a human at the gate and a record of every move.

### Category

Supervised content operations.

Not “AI copywriting.”  
Not “SEO automation.”  
Not “social scheduling.”  
Not “an AI employee.”

Galley owns the operating loop around content production.

## 4. Messaging hierarchy

### Primary message

Proof before press.

### Secondary message

Run your client content loop with a supervised agent.

### Supporting message

Every draft is checked. Every approval is recorded. Every published asset has a chain of custody.

### Product loop language

Produce → Verify → Proof → Schedule → Report

### Value language

For agency owners:

* Increase account capacity without lowering trust.
* Replace messy junior execution with a governed production line.
* Give clients a record, not excuses.

For account managers:

* Start from proof-ready drafts.
* Review only what passed verification.
* Approve, edit, reject, and schedule from one queue.

For clients:

* See what was created, approved, published, and reported.
* Trust the process without micromanaging it.

## 5. Voice and tone

Galley speaks with restraint.

Use short sentences.  
Use exact nouns.  
Use operational language.  
Avoid hype.

### Voice traits

**Editorial:** clear, polished, composed.  
**Operational:** workflow-first, specific, grounded.  
**Assured:** confident without shouting.  
**Trust-first:** always shows control, proof, and accountability.

### Good copy

“Proof-ready drafts, queued for approval.”

“Nothing publishes without a recorded human yes.”

“Every draft, edit, approval, and publish has a record.”

“Run the client content loop without rebuilding it by hand every month.”

“From playbook to proof queue to report.”

### Avoid

“Create unlimited viral content.”

“Let AI run your agency.”

“Autopilot your entire business.”

“10x your content overnight.”

“AI magic for marketers.”

“Never write again.”

The visual system should feel like a modern editorial workspace.

Think:

Not:

* crypto dashboard

## 7. Design inspiration translation

Inspired by Sana’s design language:

* huge confident headlines
* generous white/black space
* minimal navigation
* product-led cards
* short action verbs
* enterprise credibility
* precise CTAs
* calm system screenshots
* strong contrast between quiet surfaces and vivid product moments

Translated for Galley:

* editorial headlines, not AI slogans
* proofing/status surfaces, not chat-first screens
* chain-of-custody visuals, not magic sparkles
* approval queues and records as hero UI
* muted premium palette with one sharp accent
* serious trust language instead of hype

## 8. Color system

### Primary palette

**Ink Black**  
Hex: `#080807`  
Use for primary backgrounds, serious sections, nav, proof queue shell.

**Editorial White**  
Hex: `#F7F3EA`  
Use for light backgrounds, text panels, document surfaces.

**Bone**  
Hex: `#E8DFD1`  
Use for secondary backgrounds, cards, inactive surfaces.

**Charcoal**  
Hex: `#171615`  
Use for dark cards, rails, sidebars.

**Graphite**  
Hex: `#2B2926`  
Use for borders, muted surfaces, dividers.

### Accent palette

**Galley Brass**  
Hex: `#B89B5E`  
Use for primary accent, approval states, key CTA borders, selected states.

**Proof Blue**  
Hex: `#2F6BFF`  
Use sparingly for primary digital actions, links, active focus states.

**Mark Red**  
Hex: `#D94A38`  
Use for verifier flags, rejected status, critical warnings.

**Pass Green**  
Hex: `#4D8B5A`  
Use for approved, verified, published states.

**Parchment Yellow**  
Hex: `#E7C86A`  
Use for pending proof, notes, highlighted annotations.

### Usage rule

The product should be mostly black, bone, and white.  
Use brass as the signature Galley accent.  
Use blue only for interaction clarity.  
Use red/green/yellow only as system status colors.

## 9. Typography

### Typeface direction

Use a strong grotesk or neo-grotesk for the product interface and a refined editorial serif only if used with discipline.

Recommended open-source direction:

**Primary UI font:** Inter, Geist, or Satoshi-style sans  
**Editorial display option:** Fraunces, Newsreader, or Instrument Serif  
**Mono:** JetBrains Mono or IBM Plex Mono

### Type hierarchy

Hero headline:

* 72–120px desktop
* tight line-height
* strong negative space
* minimal words

Section headlines:

* 44–72px
* direct and declarative

Product labels:

* 12–14px
* uppercase or small caps
* letter-spaced
* muted color

Body:

* 16–18px
* generous line-height
* never cramped

Status text:

* short, exact, system-like

## 10. Layout principles

### Principle 1: One idea per screen

Every section must communicate one strong idea.

Bad:  
“AI-powered content, workflows, SEO, analytics, publishing, social, reports, collaboration.”

Good:  
“Nothing publishes without proof.”

### Principle 2: Product surfaces over illustrations

Show the proof queue.  
Show the record.  
Show the playbook.  
Show the report.

Do not rely on abstract AI illustrations.

### Principle 3: Use whitespace as authority

Premium software does not need to fill every inch.

Let headlines breathe.  
Let proof cards sit cleanly.  
Let the UI feel calm.

### Principle 4: Status is design

Galley’s core product is trust. Status language must be visible and beautiful.

Statuses:

* Drafting
* Verifying
* Awaiting proof
* Approved
* Scheduled
* Published
* Rejected
* Escalated

Each status should have a consistent color, icon, and microcopy pattern.

## 11. Component system

### Navigation

Minimal top nav:

* Product
* Workflow
* Security
* Pricing
* Docs
* Start a proof run

Nav should be quiet, thin, and precise.

### Hero

Hero should use a giant editorial headline.

Recommended hero:

**Proof before press.**

Subcopy:

Galley is the supervised content-operations agent for agencies and DTC teams. It produces, verifies, queues, schedules, and reports client content — with a human at the gate.

CTA:

* Start a proof run
* View the workflow

### Workflow strip

A horizontal or stacked sequence:

1. Produce  
   Drafts generated from the client playbook.

2. Verify  
   Every draft checked against voice, claims, and brand safety.

3. Proof  
   A human approves, edits, or rejects before anything moves forward.

4. Schedule  
   Approved deliverables are queued for the right channel and time.

5. Report  
   Performance and production records are assembled for the client.

### Proof queue card

Card fields:

* client name
* deliverable type
* channel
* status
* draft excerpt
* verifier result
* verifier notes
* playbook version
* approve / edit / reject
* view record

### Record timeline

The record is Galley’s trust artifact.

Timeline events:

* playbook version selected
* draft generated
* verifier passed/failed
* human edited
* human approved
* scheduled
* published
* report generated

### Client playbook panel

Sections:

* voice
* offers
* approved claims
* forbidden claims
* channels
* cadence
* examples
* reporting KPI
* last updated
* version history

### Report card

Report should feel like an executive memo, not a dashboard dump.

Sections:

* published this period
* content status
* performance snapshot
* approvals summary
* exceptions
* next recommendations

## 12. Interaction design

Motion should be restrained.

Use:

* soft fades
* subtle slide-up on cards
* precise hover states
* progressive disclosure
* calm loading states

Avoid:

* bouncing elements
* spinning AI icons
* excessive gradients
* animated sparkles
* fake typing effects
* noisy cursor effects

## 13. Product UX principles

### The proof queue is the product

The dashboard is secondary. The approval queue is where trust is created.

### The record is the moat

Every event should feel retrievable, exportable, and client-safe.

### The human gate must be visible

Never hide the approval boundary. It is not friction; it is the selling point.

### The AI should feel supervised, not mystical

Users should always know:

* what generated the draft
* what checked it
* what failed
* who approved it
* where it went

### No fake metrics

Do not show fake traffic, fake growth, fake SEO scores, or fake revenue numbers unless clearly marked as sample/demo.

## 17. Brand rules

Do:

* Lead with proof.
* Show the workflow.
* Use product UI as the hero.
* Keep copy exact.
* Make approval feel powerful.
* Make the record feel valuable.

Do not:

* Say AI replaces the human.
* Promise autonomous publishing.
* Use generic AI blobs.
* Lead with a prompt box.
* Fake performance data.
* Use “magic” as the core claim.
* Copy Sana’s visual assets, layouts, or exact language.
