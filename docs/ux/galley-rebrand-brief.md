# Galley Rebrand and UX Brief

## Brand idea

Galley is the calm checkpoint between draft and distribution. The name comes from editorial proofing, not maritime decoration. Its posture is precise, composed, and accountable.

**Product:** Galley  
**Tagline:** Proof before press.  
**Positioning:** The supervised content-ops agent for agencies and DTC teams.

## Experience principles

1. **Evidence before action.** Put verifier findings and playbook context next to every proof decision.
2. **Human authority is visible.** The interface never implies that verification or approval happened invisibly.
3. **State is plain language.** Prefer “Needs proof” and “Changes requested” over clever system names.
4. **Restraint signals confidence.** Use whitespace, rules, and typography instead of glow, glass, or decorative motion.
5. **Records are part of the work.** Audit history is a primary action, not compliance hidden in settings.

## Visual direction

- Dark ink foundation with warm bone text.
- Muted brass/parchment accent used for focus and primary actions.
- Fine, high-precision borders and mostly opaque surfaces.
- Editorial serif display type paired with a neutral sans-serif UI face.
- Tabular or monospaced numerals for run IDs, timestamps, and statuses.
- Square-to-soft corners; avoid pill-shaped containers except compact status chips.
- Motion is limited to short opacity and position transitions that explain state change.

## Implementation palette

- Ink Black `#080807` — primary background and proof queue shell.
- Editorial White `#F7F3EA` — primary text and document surfaces.
- Bone `#E8DFD1` — secondary text and quiet light surfaces.
- Charcoal `#171615` — cards, rails, and sidebars.
- Graphite `#2B2926` — borders and dividers.
- Galley Brass `#B89B5E` — signature accent and selected states.
- Proof Blue `#2F6BFF` — links, focus, and digital interaction clarity only.
- Mark Red `#D94A38` — rejected, critical, and verifier blocks.
- Pass Green `#4D8B5A` — verified, approved, and published.
- Parchment Yellow `#E7C86A` — awaiting proof, annotations, and warnings.

Status language is standardized as Drafting, Verifying, Awaiting proof, Approved, Scheduled, Published, Rejected, and Escalated.

## Avoid

- Electric blue gradients, glowing orbs, glass panels, and cartoon agents.
- “Magic,” “self-driving,” “while you sleep,” and unsupported velocity claims.
- Fake charts, vanity metrics, and decorative scores.
- Approval controls that look equivalent to publishing controls.
- Ambiguous green checks without supporting evidence.

## Key surfaces

### Landing

Lead with “Proof-ready content operations for agencies.” Explain the governed loop, then the three-part trust layer: verifier, human gate, audit trail. The primary CTA is “Start a proof run.”

### Client playbook setup

Use three chapters: account, guardrails, and measurement. Explain why each input will matter during verification. Keep setup practical and allow plain text rather than premature schema complexity.

### Proof queue

Treat the queue as an editorial desk. Each card shows the draft, verifier outcome, notes, current state, and accountable actions. Use warnings selectively. “View record” is always available.

## Voice and microcopy

- Direct: “2 items need proof.”
- Specific: “One approved claim needs a source in the draft.”
- Responsible: “Approval clears this version for scheduling. It does not publish it.”
- Calm: “No verifier blocks” rather than “Perfect score.”

## Accessibility baseline

- Meet WCAG AA contrast for body text, controls, borders, and statuses.
- Do not rely on color alone for verifier outcomes.
- Preserve visible keyboard focus and meaningful button labels.
- Use semantic headings, form labels, and live regions for action feedback.
